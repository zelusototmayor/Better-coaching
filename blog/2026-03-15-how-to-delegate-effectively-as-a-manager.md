The most common management complaint from senior leaders reviewing their teams is this: "They're still doing the work instead of leading."

Not because the managers don't know they should delegate. They know. They've been told in every leadership training they've ever attended. The problem is they don't know how to delegate in a way that actually works — that gets good outcomes, develops the person, and doesn't create more work for the manager when things go sideways.

This guide is about how, not just what.

## Why Managers Fail to Delegate

Before getting to the framework, it's worth being honest about the reasons delegation breaks down — because the fix depends on the root cause.

**"I can do it faster myself."**
Often true in the short term. Delegating takes time upfront — explaining context, answering questions, reviewing output. But this is a false economy. Every task you keep doing yourself is a task you'll always need to do. Every task you delegate properly is one your team eventually owns.

**"I don't trust them to get it right."**
This is sometimes a legitimate calibration issue (the task requires a skill the person doesn't have yet). More often, it's fear of losing control over the outcome — especially if the manager is newer to their role and still deriving identity from doing the work well.

**"If it goes wrong, it reflects on me."**
Yes. That's what management accountability means. Your job isn't to do everything yourself so nothing goes wrong. Your job is to build a team capable of doing things well, which requires giving them the opportunity — and sometimes the experience of falling short and learning from it.

**"They already have too much on."**
Sometimes this is true. But often, managers say this without actually knowing their team's workload. And even when it's true, the answer is reprioritisation, not permanent accumulation of tasks.

**"I feel guilty offloading work."**
This is common with managers who made the individual-contributor-to-manager transition recently. Delegation starts to feel like taking advantage of people who are "working so hard." But this misunderstands the role — a manager who doesn't delegate is hoarding growth opportunities, not protecting their team.

Knowing your specific failure mode helps you target the fix.

## The Delegation Framework: TASK

A useful structure for delegating well has four components:

**T — Task definition**
What is the actual deliverable? Not "help with the project" — that's an instruction, not a delegation. A well-defined task has:
- A clear output (what done looks like)
- A deadline or timeframe
- Any relevant constraints (budget, stakeholders, non-negotiables)

**A — Authority level**
What decisions can the person make independently? This is the step most managers skip, and it causes the most friction.

There are five common authority levels:
1. Gather information only — report back before taking any action
2. Analyse and recommend — suggest a course of action, but get approval before proceeding
3. Decide and inform — make the call, but tell me what you decided
4. Full authority — make the decision and tell me if something significant changes
5. Complete ownership — handle it end to end, escalate only if blocked

Making this explicit prevents two problems: the manager feeling blindsided when the person made a decision they expected to be consulted on, and the person feeling paralysed because they don't know what decisions they can make without approval.

**S — Success criteria**
What does a good outcome actually look like? How will you know the task was done well?

Without this, there are two failure modes. The person produces something technically complete that doesn't meet your actual expectations, because your real standards were in your head. Or the person asks for constant direction because they don't have an internal compass for "good enough."

Concrete success criteria might be: "The report should be readable in 5 minutes, include the top three risks with owner and date, and be ready 24 hours before the board meeting for my review." That's measurable and clear.

**K — Knowledge transfer**
What context, relationships, access, or background does the person need to actually succeed?

This is where most delegations fail in practice. The manager hands over the task without giving the person the mental model that makes good judgment possible. Then when the person's judgment is off, the manager concludes they "can't trust" them — but the person was never set up to make good calls.

Context to consider passing on:
- Why this task matters (the strategic intent, not just the instructions)
- Who the key stakeholders are and what their preferences or sensitivities are
- What's been tried before, and what failed
- What level of quality is genuinely required vs. what's good enough
- Any political dynamics or history that affects the work

## Calibrating the Authority Level

The authority level you assign should match two factors: the stakes of the decision, and the person's demonstrated capability.

| Capability | Low Stakes | High Stakes |
|---|---|---|
| Still developing | Recommend and approve | Gather and report |
| Competent | Decide and inform | Recommend and approve |
| Expert | Full authority | Decide and inform |

This calibration isn't static. As someone demonstrates good judgment in low-stakes situations, you expand their authority in higher-stakes ones. This is what "building trust" actually means in practice — it's not about whether you like the person or believe in them abstractly. It's about a track record of demonstrated judgment in increasingly complex situations.

The mistake managers make is either starting everyone at "gather and report" (which is disempowering and slow) or jumping to "full authority" without enough track record (which leads to avoidable failures and loss of confidence on both sides).

## The Feedback Loop: How to Follow Up Without Micromanaging

Once you've delegated, the challenge is staying informed without reverting to control.

**Set a check-in structure upfront.** Don't wait for problems to surface — build in checkpoints as part of the delegation. "Let's do a 15-minute check-in on Thursday to see where things are" is not micromanaging. It's accountability. The key is that the structure is agreed upfront, not imposed reactively.

**Ask questions, don't give directions.** "What's your approach here?" is very different from "Here's what you should do." Even when you can see a better path, asking the person to walk you through their thinking first has two benefits: it might reveal something you missed, and it builds their problem-solving capability rather than their dependency on yours.

**Resist the urge to take it back.** When a delegation starts looking shaky, the instinctive manager response is to rescue it — step in, take over, or quietly redo the work. This is the worst outcome for both parties. It signals to the person that they can't trust you to let them struggle and learn; it signals to you that delegation doesn't work.

Instead: surface the problem in conversation, diagnose whether it's a capability issue, a context issue, or a task definition issue, and fix that.

**Acknowledge the outcome.** When a delegation goes well, say so specifically. "You handled the client call better than I would have" is a meaningful piece of feedback that builds confidence for the next delegation. When it goes less well, do the same analysis — what can be learned, not who should be blamed.

## The Tasks You Should Never Delegate

Delegation has real limits. Some things you should always hold:

**Performance feedback.** Your direct reports need to hear significant feedback from you, not through an intermediary. Asking a peer to deliver a message you're uncomfortable with is not delegation — it's avoidance.

**Representation in key relationships.** You can send someone to a meeting on your behalf. You shouldn't systematically disappear from your own key stakeholder relationships. The principal still needs to be visible.

**Decisions that directly affect individuals' careers.** Compensation, promotion, role changes — these have a personal significance that shouldn't be handled at arm's length. The person deserves to hear it from you.

**Setting the direction of the team.** Strategy and priority calls belong to you. You can involve the team in shaping them, but abdication isn't delegation.

**Culture and values decisions.** When something happens that has implications for how the team operates, what's acceptable, what the norms are — these are not delegable.

## Building a Delegation Practice

Effective delegation isn't a one-off decision. It's a practice — something you do deliberately and continuously.

**Audit your current task list.** Go through everything you're doing this week. Ask: is this something only I can do? If not — why am I doing it?

**Match tasks to development goals.** The best delegations develop the person. If someone on your team is trying to build project management skills, giving them a project to own (with appropriate oversight) is better for both of you than delegating a repetitive task they already know how to do.

**Create a delegation cadence.** Every time you take on a new task, ask: should this be mine? Some managers build a habit of reviewing their to-do list weekly and identifying one thing to hand off.

**Talk to your team about what they want more of.** People often have a clear sense of what they'd like to own more of — and a clear sense of what they'd rather not. Ask. The delegation conversation doesn't have to be top-down.

## FAQ

**How do you delegate to someone who isn't confident taking on new tasks?**
Start with lower-stakes tasks at a "decide and inform" authority level — enough that they have to make real decisions, but not so much that a wrong call is catastrophic. Build in more check-in points than you normally would, and be explicit: "I'm giving you this to develop the skill, and we'll work through any problems together." Over time, as confidence builds, expand the scope.

**What do you do when a team member doesn't deliver on something you've delegated?**
Don't just take it back. Have the conversation: what happened, what got in the way, what would they do differently? The answer determines what comes next. If it's a capability gap — build the skill before delegating at that level again. If it was context they didn't have — fix the knowledge transfer. If it was commitment — that's a different kind of conversation.

**How much should you check in on delegated tasks without micromanaging?**
The right check-in frequency depends on the person's track record and the stakes of the task. A new team member working on a high-stakes deliverable might need daily check-ins. An experienced person on a routine task might not need a check-in at all. Set the expectation at the start, and let the person tell you if they need more or less contact.

**Is it ever okay to do a task yourself instead of delegating?**
Yes — when time is genuinely the constraint, when the specific task requires your unique authority or relationship, or when it's a one-off that won't recur. The question to ask is: is this genuinely mine to do, or is it mine because I haven't delegated it yet?

**How do you handle a team member who wants more authority than you're comfortable giving?**
Be honest about the calibration: "I want to get there — here's what I need to see first." Name the specific track record or capability that would change your comfort level. Then follow through. If you're always finding a new reason not to expand authority, that's a different problem.
