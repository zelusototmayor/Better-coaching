## Why "Difficult" Employees Are Usually a Feedback Problem

Before we get to the techniques, let's reframe the problem.

When we call an employee "difficult," we usually mean one of three things:

1. **They're underperforming** and don't seem to recognise it
2. **Their attitude is affecting the team** and they're either unaware or indifferent
3. **They push back on feedback** and conversations with them feel like an argument you can't win

In all three cases, there's a feedback loop that's either broken or missing. The employee is operating on a different map of reality than the one you're working from. They either don't know what's expected, don't know they're falling short, don't know the impact of their behaviour — or some combination of all three.

This matters because it changes the approach. The goal isn't to "win" the conversation. It's to close the gap between their map and yours — and create a shared understanding of what needs to change and why.

---

## The Most Common Feedback Mistakes Managers Make with Difficult Employees

Before the framework, it helps to name what doesn't work:

**Waiting too long.** Most managers avoid the conversation until the situation has deteriorated past the point where a single conversation can fix it. By the time feedback is given, the employee has been doing the problematic thing for months — and the manager has built up months of frustration. The conversation happens too hot, too late, and with too much baggage. Give feedback when the pattern is first clear, not when you've reached your limit.

**Being vague to avoid conflict.** "Your attitude has been a bit off lately" is not feedback. It's a complaint. The employee doesn't know what specifically they should do differently, and the vagueness gives them room to dismiss it ("I'm just having a hard week"). Specific behaviour, specific impact — or it's not a feedback conversation, it's a mood.

**Making it about character.** "You're not a team player" is an attack on identity. "When you don't share your updates in the weekly sync, the team can't plan effectively" is feedback on behaviour. People can change behaviour. They can't change who they are — and telling them who they are puts them on the defensive immediately.

**Giving feedback and then disappearing.** A one-time conversation that isn't followed up produces one-time change at best. Feedback without follow-through teaches the employee that if they weather the initial conversation, nothing will actually happen.

**Avoiding the conversation by cc'ing more people.** Adding HR, their skip-level manager, or other stakeholders to communications before you've had a direct conversation is escalation without resolution. It signals to the employee that you're building a case, not trying to help them — and it makes the actual conversation much harder when it eventually happens.

## The SBI-Impact Framework for Difficult Feedback

For feedback with a difficult employee, you need structure. Structure removes the personal heat and creates a conversation that's about observable facts rather than interpretations or judgements.

The SBI-Impact model:

- **S — Situation:** When and where did this happen? Be specific.
- **B — Behaviour:** What specifically did the person do or say? Observable action only — not interpretation.
- **I — Impact:** What happened as a result? What effect did this have on the team, the project, the customer?
- **Change needed:** What specifically needs to be different going forward?

### Example: The Disengaged Team Member

❌ Vague: "You've been pretty checked out lately and it's affecting the team."

✅ SBI: "In last Thursday's team retrospective [Situation], you left after the first 15 minutes without explanation [Behaviour]. The team had to pause the session and we weren't able to reach a decision on the deployment timeline [Impact]. Going forward, I need you to be present for the full retro — if there's a conflict, let me know in advance."

### Example: The Defensive Responder

❌ Vague: "You make it really hard to give you feedback."

✅ SBI: "When I reviewed your project proposal on Tuesday and gave you three specific change requests [Situation], you sent a 12-paragraph email explaining why each one was wrong [Behaviour]. I need to be able to give notes without having to defend each one — the back-and-forth delays the project and it means other reviewers are less likely to engage with your work [Impact]. What I'd like instead is for you to take feedback in, make the changes or raise specific questions, and keep the response short."

### Example: The Attitude Problem

❌ Vague: "Your attitude has been really negative and it's bringing the team down."

✅ SBI: "In the all-hands on Monday [Situation], when the new product direction was announced, you said out loud 'we've been down this road before and it never works' [Behaviour]. Two people came to me afterwards saying they were demoralized and didn't want to work on the initiative [Impact]. I need you to raise concerns with me directly in our 1:1 — not in front of the team in all-hands settings."

## Before the Conversation: Prepare These Four Things

**1. The specific examples you'll use.**  
Write them down. Three examples is the right number — enough to establish a pattern, not so many that it feels like a prosecution. Pick the most recent and clearest examples. Older examples let the employee claim things have changed.

**2. Your opening sentence.**  
The first 30 seconds determine whether the conversation is productive or defensive. Don't start with a compliment sandwich — it signals that something hard is coming and puts people on alert. Start direct but non-aggressive: "I want to talk about [specific situation] because it's important and I think we need to get on the same page." Neutral, specific, non-threatening.

**3. The change you're asking for.**  
Be precise about what success looks like. Not "I need you to be more engaged" but "I need you at every team meeting unless you've let me know in advance, and I need you to contribute at least one specific idea per session." Ambiguous outcomes give difficult employees room to claim they've changed without actually changing anything.

**4. What happens if nothing changes.**  
Know this before you walk in. You don't necessarily have to say it in the first conversation, but you need to know — so that if the employee asks "what happens if I don't change this?" you're not caught off guard. The consequence needs to be real, proportionate, and something you're actually prepared to follow through on.

## During the Conversation: How to Handle Pushback

Difficult employees are usually difficult in feedback conversations too. Here are the common defensive moves and how to handle them:

### "That's not what happened."

Don't argue about facts. Acknowledge the difference in perception: "You may have experienced it differently — I'm telling you how it came across to me and to others in the room. Regardless of the intent, the impact was [X]. That's what we need to address."

### "Other people do that too. Why are you only talking to me?"

This is a deflection, not a counterargument. "I'm here talking to you right now. If other people have the same issue, I'll address it with them separately. Right now I'm focused on this specific behaviour."

### "You're being unfair."

Don't defend yourself. Ask: "Help me understand what specifically feels unfair." Then listen. Either you'll learn something about how the feedback landed (useful), or the employee will struggle to articulate it specifically (which often defuses the charge). Don't capitulate just to reduce tension — but do listen for whether the accusation has merit.

### Silence or emotional shutdown.

Some people go quiet when receiving hard feedback. Don't fill the silence by walking back what you said. Give them space: "Take a moment — this is a lot." Then, when they're ready: "What are your thoughts?" If they're too shut down to engage, schedule a follow-up: "Let's pick this up in a couple of days when you've had time to process."

### "I've been stressed / going through a lot personally."

This deserves empathy without becoming an excuse: "I hear you, and I appreciate you sharing that. I still need [behaviour X] to change — but let's also talk about what support you might need to make that easier." Personal circumstances explain behaviour; they don't exempt people from it.

## The Follow-Up: What Happens After the Conversation

The conversation itself is the beginning, not the solution. Here's what makes the feedback stick:

**Document what was agreed.** After the meeting, send a brief summary: "Thanks for the conversation earlier. To summarise what we agreed: [specific change], starting [specific date]. I'll check in with you on [date]." This isn't punitive — it's clarity. It removes the "I didn't realise you were being serious" defence.

**Check in within one week.** Don't wait for the monthly review. A brief, low-key check-in ("How's it going with [specific thing]?") signals that you're watching and that you care. It also gives the employee an opportunity to report progress, which reinforces the change.

**Acknowledge improvement immediately.** If the behaviour changes, name it. "I noticed you were fully present in the retrospective this week — that's exactly what I needed." Difficult employees rarely get specific positive feedback. When behaviour changes and no one acknowledges it, the change often doesn't last.

**Escalate if nothing changes.** If three conversations haven't produced change, the nature of the conversation shifts — from coaching to a performance improvement process. Be honest with the employee: "We've talked about this three times. I haven't seen the change I need. This is now a formal performance conversation and the consequences are [X]." This is not a failure of the feedback process. Some employees won't change through coaching alone, and knowing that clearly is itself useful information.

## A Note on Difficult vs. Underperforming

These are different problems that sometimes look the same. A genuinely difficult employee — someone who undermines, deflects, and creates friction as a pattern — may need a different intervention than someone who is underperforming due to unclear expectations, skill gaps, or personal challenges.

Before assuming "difficult," ask:

- **Do they know what's expected?** If expectations haven't been made explicit, the first conversation is about clarity, not performance.
- **Do they have what they need to succeed?** Underperformance driven by resource constraints or skill gaps requires a different response than attitude-driven underperformance.
- **Is this pattern or circumstance?** A typically strong employee going through a rough period deserves empathy and support. A chronically difficult employee who only performs when someone is watching them needs a different approach.

The framework above works for both — but the tone and the goal shift depending on which situation you're in.

## FAQ

**How do I give feedback to someone who always has an excuse?**  
Acknowledge the excuse, then return to the behaviour: "I understand there was [external factor]. The impact on the team was still [X], and that's what I need to address with you. Going forward, if [external factor] is a risk, what can you do differently?" The goal is to separate explanation from accountability. An excuse explains what happened; it doesn't change what needs to happen next time.

**What if the feedback conversation makes things worse?**  
Sometimes it does, in the short term. An employee who responds to feedback with withdrawal, passive aggression, or increased friction is giving you information — about whether the relationship is recoverable, and about whether the conversation approach needs to change. Document what happened, consult HR if the behaviour escalates, and don't interpret a bad initial reaction as a reason to stop giving feedback.

**How often should I be giving feedback to a difficult employee?**  
More often than you're doing it, probably. Waiting until the situation is severe means conversations are high-stakes and heavily loaded. Frequent, low-key, specific feedback about specific behaviours creates a culture of normal course-correction rather than special-event confrontation.

**Should I involve HR before the conversation?**  
For the first conversation about a behaviour issue, generally no — involving HR immediately signals that you're building a formal case rather than trying to resolve the issue directly. Involve HR when: the behaviour is severe (harassment, insubordination), you've had multiple direct conversations without change, or you're moving toward a formal performance improvement process.

**How do I give feedback when the employee is older or more experienced than me?**  
The framework is the same. The tone may need to be slightly more collegial — "I want to think through this together" rather than "here's what I need you to do" — but the substance doesn't change. Competence and experience don't exempt anyone from feedback. What changes is how you frame the ask: as collaboration rather than correction where possible.

**What's the biggest mistake managers make in feedback conversations with difficult employees?**  
Softening the message so much that the employee doesn't understand the feedback was serious. The desire to avoid conflict leads to so many qualifications, caveats, and positive observations that the core message gets lost. The employee leaves thinking it was fine. The manager leaves thinking the conversation happened. Three months later, the manager is frustrated that "nothing changed" — and the employee is blindsided by the escalation. Say clearly what needs to change.
