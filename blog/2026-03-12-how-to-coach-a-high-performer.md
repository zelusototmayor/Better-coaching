High performers are easy to mismanage. They deliver consistently, rarely complain, and require minimal day-to-day direction — so managers often leave them alone. The logic seems reasonable: why fix what isn't broken?

The problem is that high performers don't experience neglect as respect. They experience it as stagnation. And stagnation, for someone wired to grow, eventually becomes a reason to leave.

Coaching your best people is one of the highest-leverage activities a manager can do. This guide covers the specific approaches that work — and the common mistakes that push your strongest contributors toward the exit.

---

## Why High Performers Are the Most At-Risk Employees

A counterintuitive truth: your highest performers are often your most likely to leave.

Research from the Corporate Executive Council found that high performers are 25% more likely to quit than average performers. The reason is simple — they have options. When a high performer decides their current environment can't offer what they need, they have plenty of places to go.

What they need isn't usually more money (though that helps). It's growth, challenge, autonomy, and the sense that someone at the company sees and invests in their potential.

Most managers don't withhold these things deliberately. They just allocate coaching time by urgency — and high performers rarely generate urgent problems.

---

## The 4 Most Common Mistakes When Coaching High Performers

**1. Treating "no problems" as "no needs"**

The absence of visible issues doesn't mean a high performer is thriving. They may be operating well below their potential, bored by work they've outgrown, or privately frustrated by things they don't feel comfortable raising. Regular, substantive 1:1s uncover these signals before they become resignation letters.

**2. Only using them as fixers**

High performers are often pulled into firefighting — covering for gaps, rescuing struggling projects, propping up weaker teammates. This feels like trust, but over time it signals that you value their output more than their growth. Firefighting work rarely develops new skills.

**3. Withholding stretch assignments because they're "too important" to risk**

Managers sometimes protect high performers from failure by keeping them in their proven lane. This feels prudent. It's actually a ceiling. High performers need assignments that challenge them, including ones where success isn't guaranteed.

**4. Skipping feedback because the work is good**

Without feedback, high performers can't calibrate their development. "Your work is great" is not coaching. Specific, behavioral feedback — even on strong performance — helps them understand what's working, what could be stronger, and what growth looks like at the next level.

## What Effective Coaching of High Performers Actually Looks Like

### Ask about ambition, not just performance

Most coaching conversations focus backward: what happened, what went well, what could be improved. With high performers, you also need to look forward: where do they want to go? What would they want to be doing in two years? What skills are they trying to build?

These aren't annual review questions. They're regular 1:1 questions. If you don't know what your best people are trying to become, you can't help them get there — and someone else will.

**Useful prompts:**
- "What kind of work energizes you most right now?"
- "Is there a project or scope that would feel like a stretch for you?"
- "Where do you feel you're developing most? Where do you feel stuck?"

---

### Give feedback that matches their level

Feedback for a high performer is different from feedback for someone who needs to improve. Instead of corrective feedback, think about ceiling-raising feedback: what separates good from excellent? What habits or approaches would matter more as the scope of their work grows?

A senior engineer who writes clear code might receive ceiling-raising feedback about communication during cross-team projects. A strong individual contributor might hear feedback about their influence with stakeholders — a skill that matters at the next level even if it's not hurting them now.

**The frame to use:** "You're doing X well. As you grow into [next scope/role], here's what I think you'll want to develop..."

---

### Protect their time and energy, not just their output

High performers often say yes to everything. They absorb requests, take on extra work, and are reluctant to push back because they can handle it. Over time, this becomes unsustainable — and you often see it in burnout before you see it in performance.

A good coach of high performers actively manages their cognitive load. That means:
- Saying no on their behalf to low-value work
- Creating space in their schedule for deep work
- Having an explicit conversation about what's sustainable versus what's burning runway

---

### Sponsor them, not just develop them

Development is something that happens to someone — they take a course, they get feedback. Sponsorship is something you do for someone — you put their name forward, you create visibility, you advocate for them in rooms they're not in.

High performers benefit from both. But sponsorship has more impact on retention. When someone knows their manager is actively advocating for their career, leaving that manager becomes significantly harder.

Concrete sponsorship actions:
- Name them in meetings when their work is relevant
- Connect them to senior stakeholders directly
- Recommend them for high-visibility projects before they ask
- Advocate for their promotion before the cycle forces the conversation

## How to Structure a 1:1 With a High Performer

The most effective 1:1s with high performers have a different rhythm than problem-solving sessions. They're less about issue-resolution and more about forward motion.

A simple structure that works:

1. **Check-in (5 min)** — What's top of mind? What did you work on since we last talked?
2. **Project pulse (10 min)** — Quick scan of current work. Any blockers? Any wins worth noting?
3. **Development focus (10-15 min)** — One question about where they're growing or what they're trying to build. Go deep here.
4. **Anything for me (5 min)** — What do you need from me? What would be helpful that I'm not currently providing?

The fourth question is the one most managers skip. It's the most important one for high performers.

## Signs You're Coaching a High Performer Effectively

- They tell you about their ambitions, not just their blockers
- They're regularly assigned work that stretches them
- They receive specific feedback, not just validation
- You can describe what they're working toward at the next level
- They've told you what they want out of their career — and you've acted on it

---

## Signs You're Quietly Losing Them

- Your 1:1s are mostly tactical (project updates, blockers)
- You haven't had a development conversation in the last month
- They're doing the same core work they were doing a year ago
- They rarely raise ideas or push back on decisions
- You assume they're fine because they haven't complained

That last one is the most common. High performers don't complain — they update their LinkedIn.

---

## The Bottom Line

Coaching high performers requires more intentionality than most managers bring to it. The absence of visible problems creates a false sense of security. The real risk is invisible: a high performer who's decided to stay, but only for now.

Invest in regular development conversations. Give ceiling-raising feedback. Sponsor them actively. Protect their energy. Ask what they're trying to become.

The return on that investment — in retention, in performance, in team culture — is compounding. And the cost of getting it wrong walks out the door with two weeks' notice.
