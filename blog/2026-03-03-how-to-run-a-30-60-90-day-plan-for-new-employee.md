## Introduction

Most onboarding plans get created once and then ignored.

The manager sends a document on day one — context, team structure, some links to company wikis — and then assumes the hire will figure it out. Six weeks later, the hire is confused about their actual priorities, afraid to ask basic questions, and performing below what the role requires. Three months in, both sides are frustrated.

A 30-60-90 day plan is not a document. It's a conversation framework — a structured way to align expectations, check in regularly, and course-correct before small problems become hiring mistakes.

This guide covers how to build one, how to run the conversations, and what good looks like in each phase.

---

## Why 30-60-90 Day Plans Work (When Done Right)

The structure forces two things most onboarding processes skip:

**1. Explicit expectations by phase.** Instead of a vague "get up to speed," the hire knows exactly what they're supposed to understand, be able to do, and deliver — in each of the three phases. No ambiguity about whether month one is for observing or contributing.

**2. Regular checkpoints.** Managers who skip structured check-ins tend to give feedback only when something has already gone wrong. The 30, 60, and 90-day reviews create a rhythm where feedback is expected and early corrections are normal, not alarming.

Research on new hire productivity consistently shows the same finding: structured onboarding shortens time-to-full-contribution by 30-50% compared to informal onboarding. That's months, not days.

## The Structure: What Each Phase Is For

Before writing a single objective, understand what each phase is actually designed to accomplish.

### Days 1-30: Learn

The first 30 days are about orientation, not contribution. The hire's job is to understand:

- How the organisation actually works (not how the org chart says it does)
- Who does what, and who makes which decisions
- Where the information lives
- What good looks like in this role
- The unwritten rules

The manager's job in this phase is to reduce uncertainty. New hires burn significant cognitive energy on things experienced employees don't have to think about: where to find things, who to ask, what's acceptable, what's important. The faster you remove that friction, the faster they can focus on actual work.

**What to avoid:** Overloading the first 30 days with projects, deliverables, or independence. This is orientation, not sprint planning.

### Days 31-60: Apply

The second 30 days shift from learning to doing — under guidance. The hire should now be able to:

- Work on real tasks with increasing independence
- Make decisions in low-stakes areas
- Start building relationships with key stakeholders
- Surface problems and questions proactively

This is when you find out whether your learning phase actually worked. If the hire is still unclear on fundamentals at day 45, it's better to know now than at day 89.

**What to watch for:** Hires who look productive but are avoiding the hard parts of the role. Activity is not the same as output.

### Days 61-90: Contribute

By 90 days, the hire should be operating close to full capacity — contributing independently, owning outcomes, and needing less hand-holding.

This doesn't mean perfect. It means:

- Clear about priorities without needing them restated
- Proactively solving problems rather than waiting for direction
- Producing work at the quality and volume the role requires
- Integrating into the team naturally

If you're not seeing this at 90 days, you have information you need to act on — either the plan wasn't set correctly, something changed, or there's a performance issue to address early.

## How to Write the Plan

A usable 30-60-90 day plan has three components per phase:

**1. Learning goals** — What should they understand?  
**2. Performance goals** — What should they be able to do?  
**3. Relationship goals** — Who should they know, and what relationships matter?

Write these collaboratively with the hire, not for them. The plan is more effective when the new employee has had input — they'll take ownership rather than treating it as a manager's document.

### Example: Account Manager Role

**Days 1-30**

*Learning:*
- Understand the top 10 accounts: history, current status, key contacts
- Shadow at least 3 client calls
- Complete product training
- Learn the CRM workflow and internal reporting process

*Performance:*
- Handle all admin for 2 accounts independently by end of week 4
- Complete the new hire certification assessment

*Relationships:*
- 1:1 introductions with each team member
- Meet the key contacts at 5 top accounts
- Relationship established with the Customer Success lead

**Days 31-60**

*Learning:*
- Understand renewal process and typical negotiation patterns
- Understand escalation paths for account issues

*Performance:*
- Own 8-10 accounts independently
- Lead 3 client calls with manager in attendance
- Identify at least 2 upsell opportunities and document them

*Relationships:*
- First independent relationship built with a previously uncontacted stakeholder at a top account
- Check-in established with Solutions team for cross-functional support

**Days 61-90**

*Performance:*
- Carrying full account load (20 accounts)
- Quarterly business review completed for 5 accounts
- Pipeline contribution: 2 expansion deals at proposal stage
- NPS for assigned accounts at or above team average

*Relationships:*
- Attending cross-functional meetings independently
- Peer feedback: team describes new hire as a reliable collaborator

## Running the Check-In Conversations

The plan is only useful if the conversations happen — and happen well.

### The 30-Day Review

**Goal:** Confirm the hire has the foundation they need.

Questions to ask:
- What's been clearer than you expected? What's still unclear?
- What's been harder than expected?
- Do you understand what success looks like in your role?
- What do you need from me that you're not getting?

Be prepared for honesty if you've created a safe environment for it. If the hire says they're struggling to understand the priorities, that's not a red flag — it's early, useful information.

Adjust the plan if the first phase revealed gaps. 30-60-90 day plans are not carved in stone.

### The 60-Day Review

**Goal:** Address problems while there's still time.

By day 60, you should have real performance data — actual work output, quality of decisions, stakeholder relationships, initiative. Use it.

Questions to ask:
- Looking at the plan objectives: which are on track, which aren't?
- What are the obstacles you're running into most often?
- What should we adjust for the next 30 days?
- What's your confidence level going into Q3 of the plan?

If there are concerns, raise them directly at 60 days. "I've noticed X, and I want to talk about it" is easier to say and receive at 60 days than at 90 when the probation decision is approaching.

### The 90-Day Review

**Goal:** Make an honest assessment.

This is the moment to evaluate the full plan against actual outcomes. For each objective:

- Met / Not met / Exceeded
- What the evidence is
- What it means for the next phase

If the hire has met or exceeded the plan, use this conversation to set the next milestone and signal confidence. If there are significant gaps, be specific about what needs to change, and set a 30-day improvement plan with clear checkpoints.

Avoid the 90-day review that is mostly positive feedback followed by a vague "keep it up." Specificity is what makes the conversation useful.

## Common Mistakes Managers Make

**Setting objectives without context.** "Own 10 accounts independently" is meaningless if the hire doesn't know what "independently" requires or what the typical complexity of those accounts is. Define the standard, not just the target.

**Writing the plan alone.** A plan the hire hasn't seen until day one is a directive, not a framework. Write it together. The conversation itself is valuable — it surfaces assumptions on both sides.

**Skipping check-ins when things seem fine.** The 30-day conversation isn't just for catching problems. It's for catching gaps before they become problems. Skipping it when things look good is exactly when you miss the subtle issues.

**Not adjusting the plan.** If the first 30 days revealed that the role is more complex than expected, or the business changed, or the hire came with different gaps than anticipated — adjust. A plan that's rigidly enforced despite new information isn't a management tool; it's a documentation exercise.

**Treating day 90 as the finish line.** The plan gets the hire to full contribution. What happens next determines whether they stay. At 90 days, set the next 90-day objectives or a longer-term development plan. Don't let structure disappear once onboarding officially ends.

---

## When the Plan Isn't Working

If a hire is behind plan at 60 days, three questions help identify the cause:

1. **Were the expectations clear?** If objectives were vague or changed, the plan failed — not the hire.
2. **Were the resources available?** If the hire couldn't get access, support, or information they needed, that's a systems problem.
3. **Is there a genuine performance issue?** If expectations were clear, resources were available, and outcomes are still missing — that's the conversation to have directly.

Most early performance issues are expectation gaps, not talent gaps. The 30-60-90 structure is specifically designed to surface the former before concluding the latter.

**What is a 30-60-90 day plan for a new employee?**  
A 30-60-90 day plan breaks the first three months into distinct phases: learning (days 1-30), applying (days 31-60), and contributing (days 61-90). Each phase has specific goals for what the employee should understand, be able to do, and deliver.

**Should the manager or the new employee write it?**  
Both. The manager sets the framework; the new employee contributes to how they'll achieve the objectives. Plans written collaboratively have stronger buy-in and surface assumption gaps before they become problems.

**What should be included?**  
Each phase needs learning goals (what to understand), performance goals (what to deliver), and relationship goals (who to build working relationships with). Goals should be specific enough to measure.

**What happens if the employee isn't meeting the plan?**  
First, identify why. Most gaps are caused by unclear expectations or missing resources — not talent issues. Adjust the plan if the context changed. If expectations were clear and well-supported, address it directly at 60 days while there's time to course-correct.

**How is it different from a probation review?**  
A probation review is one conversation at 3 or 6 months. A 30-60-90 plan has structured check-ins at each milestone, so problems surface at 30 and 60 days — when there's still time to fix them — not at 90 days when the decision is due.

**What should happen after 90 days?**  
Set the next milestone: quarterly OKRs, a 6-month performance framework, or a development plan. Structured expectations shouldn't disappear when onboarding ends.
