Every manager has one of these conversations coming. Maybe it's the team member who's been missing deadlines and blaming everyone else. Maybe it's the high performer who's started snapping at colleagues. Maybe it's the person who doesn't know their job is at risk if things don't change.

Most managers deal with difficult conversations in one of two ways: they avoid them until the situation becomes a formal process, or they have them poorly — blurting something out in frustration, being vague to avoid conflict, or over-scripting it into a robotic HR ritual.

Neither works. Here's what does.

---

## Why Difficult Conversations Feel So Hard

Understanding why you avoid these conversations makes them easier to have.

**Fear of the emotional response.** Nobody wants to make someone cry, angry, or defensive. But managing that fear by not saying the hard thing costs more — both in the relationship and in the actual performance problem.

**Uncertainty about what to say.** Without a clear framework, difficult conversations feel like they could go anywhere. The uncertainty of that is deeply uncomfortable.

**Worry about damaging the relationship.** Counterintuitively, the relationship usually survives honest conversations much better than prolonged avoidance. People know when something is wrong. Naming it often creates more trust than ignoring it.

**Imposter syndrome about authority.** Newer managers especially struggle with the legitimacy question — "who am I to tell them this?" But your role is precisely to provide feedback and hold standards. That's the job.

The goal isn't to feel comfortable. It's to do it anyway.

## The Pre-Conversation Preparation

The conversation itself is 20% of the work. The preparation is 80%.

### 1. Be clear on the actual problem

Before you say anything to the person, be precise about what you're addressing. Not "attitude" — that's too vague to act on. Not "performance" — that could mean anything.

What specifically happened? What was the impact? What needs to change?

Write it down in one or two sentences. If you can't write it clearly, you're not ready to have the conversation.

Examples of clear vs. vague:

❌ "Your attitude in meetings has been off lately."
✅ "In the last three project reviews, you interrupted two colleagues and contradicted the agreed plan in front of the client. This is affecting team confidence and client trust."

❌ "Your work quality needs to improve."
✅ "Three of the last five reports you submitted had factual errors that required rework before client delivery. This is happening more frequently than three months ago."

### 2. Separate observation from interpretation

Before the conversation, distinguish between what you directly observed (facts) and what you're interpreting from those facts.

Observable: "You haven't responded to emails within the agreed 24-hour window for three weeks."
Interpretation: "You don't care about the team's needs."

You can state the observation confidently. The interpretation may or may not be accurate — and starting with an interpretation triggers defensiveness immediately.

Start with what you observed. Ask about the interpretation.

### 3. Know what outcome you want

What would success look like after this conversation? Is it:
- Specific behaviour stopping or changing?
- A clearer mutual understanding of expectations?
- A documented agreement that forms part of a performance process?
- A repair in a working relationship?

Without knowing the outcome you want, the conversation meanders. With it, you can stay anchored.

### 4. Choose the right setting

Private, face-to-face (or video if remote), adequate time (minimum 30 minutes for anything significant), and not right after an incident when emotions are hot.

Never spring it on someone in passing. "Can I have a quick word?" followed by a significant performance message isn't respectful and doesn't get good results.

## The Conversation Framework: SBI-C

The SBI-C model is a reliable structure for difficult conversations that reduces defensiveness and keeps the conversation productive.

**S — Situation:** Set the specific context. When, where, what circumstance.
**B — Behaviour:** Describe the observable behaviour, not the interpretation.
**I — Impact:** State the impact of that behaviour — on the team, the work, the relationship, the outcome.
**C — Curiosity:** Ask a genuine question to understand their perspective before you move to what needs to change.

Example in practice:

"In Tuesday's team meeting [Situation], you interrupted Sarah twice while she was presenting and then contradicted her recommendation in front of the client without flagging it to me beforehand [Behaviour]. The client looked confused, and Sarah looked embarrassed — I could see it affected the room [Impact]. Help me understand what was happening for you in that moment [Curiosity]."

The Curiosity step is the most important and most skipped. It does three things:
1. It signals that you're not just delivering a verdict — you're trying to understand
2. It sometimes reveals context you weren't aware of (they got terrible news that morning; there's a conflict with Sarah you didn't know about)
3. It moves the person from defensive mode to reflective mode

After the curiosity exchange, move to what needs to change:

"What I need to see going forward is [specific behaviour change]. Does that make sense, and is there anything getting in the way of that?"

## Handling the Most Common Pushback Responses

**"You're wrong / that's not what happened"**

Don't argue about whether your observation is accurate. Return to what you observed: "I understand you see it differently. What I observed was [X]. Can you walk me through what you were thinking in that moment?"

You're not there to win the factual argument. You're there to create enough shared understanding that the behaviour can change.

**"You're picking on me / this is unfair"**

Acknowledge the feeling without backing down from the substance: "I hear that this feels unfair, and that matters. I'm raising this because I want you to be successful here, not because I'm looking to build a case. Let's make sure we're both clear on what I'm asking for going forward."

**Silence / shutting down**

Give space. Don't fill every silence with more words. Ask: "What are you thinking right now?" — and wait for the answer.

**Deflecting with other people's behaviour**

"But [colleague] does the same thing" is a deflection, not a defence. Respond: "I can only speak to what I've observed with you in this conversation. If there are things others are doing that concern you, I want to hear about that separately. For now, can we stay focused on this?"

**Tears or strong emotion**

Acknowledge the emotion and give it space: "I can see this is hitting hard. Take a minute." Don't rush past it. Don't apologise for raising the topic. Once they've had a moment, check whether they're ready to continue.

## The Specific Difficult Conversations Managers Avoid Most

### Performance problems

Be explicit that this is a performance conversation, not just a check-in. Vagueness is unkind here — people need to know the stakes.

Use the SBI-C framework to be specific. Agree on a clear next step: what changes, by when, and how you'll know it has. Document it.

### Behaviour affecting others

The same framework applies, but lead more strongly with impact on the team. Individual behaviour that affects others is a team problem, not just a personal feedback issue.

### "You may be at risk if this doesn't change"

If someone's job genuinely depends on a change, say so. Euphemisms like "this is a concern" leave people unaware of the actual stakes. You don't need to threaten — you need to be honest: "I want to be direct with you: if this pattern continues, it puts your position here at risk. I'm telling you this because I want you to have the full picture."

### Interpersonal conflict between team members

Don't mediate blindly. Have individual conversations first, using SBI-C with each person. Understand both sides before bringing people together. A premature group conversation escalates rather than resolves.

## After the Conversation

**Document what was discussed and agreed.** A brief email summary ("Following our conversation today, we agreed that...") is not HR bureaucracy — it's clarity. Both parties have the same record of what was said and what changes are expected.

**Follow through on your part.** If you said you'd provide more support, provide it. If you said you'd check in in two weeks, check in. Trust is built on whether you do what you say.

**Don't make it weird after.** The conversation happened. The relationship continues. Don't over-compensate with excessive warmth or become cold and formal. Treat the person normally.

**Notice and name improvement.** When things change in the right direction, say so. Positive reinforcement is part of the framework, not just a performance management nicety.

## FAQ

**How do you start a difficult conversation without it feeling abrupt?**
Be direct but human. "There's something I need to address with you, and I want to do it respectfully" is a fine opener. Trying to ease into it with small talk before pivoting to the hard thing usually makes it more awkward. Say what you're there for.

**What if the person becomes very emotional or the conversation breaks down?**
Pause the conversation. "I can see this is a lot to process. Let's take a break and pick this up [tomorrow / at X time]." Don't abandon the conversation entirely — it needs to be completed. But forcing it when someone is in acute distress doesn't work.

**How long should a difficult conversation be?**
Most substantive difficult conversations need at least 30 minutes. Rushing them doesn't produce resolution. Budget an hour if there's genuine complexity. A follow-up conversation is often needed anyway.

**Is it better to prepare a script or be spontaneous?**
Prepare the key points (what you observed, the impact, what needs to change) but don't script it word for word. Scripts read as scripts and make the conversation feel like a procedure rather than a human interaction.

**What if the manager is the problem and the employee wants to raise it?**
That's a separate dynamic — upward feedback and conflict with your manager — and it deserves its own guide. The SBI-C principles still apply when giving upward feedback, but the power dynamic requires different framing.
